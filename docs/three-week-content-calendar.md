### Monday, July 20 — Blank-page friction

Content ID: `w1_mon_blank_page`

**LinkedIn**

The hardest part of journaling is often not writing.

It is deciding where to begin when your mind already feels full.

“Write about your day” sounds simple, but it quietly asks you to choose the subject, decide what matters, find the right words, and work out when to stop.

A smaller starting point works better:

**What is taking up more mental space than it deserves today?**

Write three sentences. Do not solve it. Just make it concrete.

What usually stops you from journaling: starting, finding time, or staying consistent?

## Week 2 — Demonstrate the method and trust boundary

### Monday, July 27 — Three-line entry

Content ID: `w2_mon_three_lines`

**LinkedIn**

If journaling feels like a 30-minute task, make it a three-line task.

1. What happened?
2. What am I telling myself about it?
3. What do I want to remember tomorrow?

The point is not to produce beautiful writing. The point is to leave yourself something concrete to return to.

Save this for the next time a blank page feels too large.

## Week 3 — Invite participation and surface objections

### Monday, August 3 — Consistency without streak pressure

Content ID: `w3_mon_consistency`

**LinkedIn**

Missing one day is not proof that a journaling habit failed.

Rigid streaks can turn reflection into a score. A better rule for a short experiment is: return to the next available prompt and keep the earlier work.

MindFlow’s seven-day experience can be completed across a ten-day window. Previously unlocked entries stay available.

The goal is to return, not to protect a perfect number.

What helps you resume a habit after missing a day?
